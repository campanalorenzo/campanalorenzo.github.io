  3. Stochastic modelling of non-spherical particles in turbulence
    Campana, Lorenzo
    PhD. Thesis Mar 2022
    Abs HAL PDF

    The thesis focuses on the dynamics of anisotropic particles in turbulence exploiting stochastic Lagrangian methods. The development of models that reach an acceptable compromise between simplicity and accuracy and that can be used as predictive tools in industrial computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is needed for progressing in the field of medical, environmental and industrial processes. A stochastic orientation model is studied in two-dimensional turbulent flow with homogeneous shear; the results are compared with direct numerical simulations (DNS). We look for analytical results, by scrutinising the effect of the anisotropies and by extending the notion of rotational dynamics in the stochastic framework. The two-dimensional model is then extended to the three-dimensional case. We introduce a numerical scheme which is able to to preserve the dynamics at reasonable computational costs and we analyse its convergence. We develop a scheme of splitting decomposition of the stochastic differential equations (SDE), in order to overcome the typical instability problems of the Euler–Maruyama method; we obtain a mean-square strong convergence of order 1/2 and a weak convergence of order 1. Finally, the model and the numerical scheme are implemented in an industrial CFD code (Code Saturne) and they are used to study the orientational and rotational behaviour of anisotropic inertia-free particles in a turbulent channel flow. The stochastic Lagrangian model for the orientation in the CFD code reproduces with some limits the orientation and rotation statistics of the DNS. The results obtained on the orientation and rotation of aspherical particles in turbulence give new insight into large scale motions both, in two-dimensional space of interest for geophysical flows, and in three-dimensional industrial applications.

  1. Turbulent Drag Reduction by SHSs.
    Campana, Lorenzo
    MSc. Thesis Mar 2017
    Abs PDF

    The superhydrophobic surfaces (SHSs) were initially inspired by the unique water-repellent properties of the lotus leaf and can be employed to produce drag reduction in turbulent flows. The effects of a SHS consist on micro-grooves which can entrap gas pockets when sub-merged in the water, i.e. at the groove an interface liquid-gas with a no zero velocity is present. In this work, direct numerical simulations (DNS) are used to investigate the drag-reducing performance of SHS in a turbulent pipe flow. The configuration of SHS influences the drag reduction within the pipe. The drag appears reduced in the SHS cases with respect to the smooth classical case. This effect strongly depends on the geometrical features of the surfaces. The increase of drag reduction is remarkable in the cases with a large groove width. To understand the phenomena linked to this mechanism, the turbulent vortical structures are studied. These last are significantly modified compared to the smooth case. The result obtained in this work underlines that the contribution of the Reynolds stress within the pipe rises with the width of groove, providing a negative contribution to the drag reduction. This effect is exceeded by the contribution of the slip velocity at the wall. SHS technology represents an emergent application in many fields of research and in a broad spectrum of industrial and everyday life applications.
